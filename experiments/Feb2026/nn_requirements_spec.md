
# Latent Manifold Discovery from GEM‑LTE Indices  
*Requirements Specification for a Neural‑Network Numerical Experiment*  

## 1. Objective

Design and execute a neural‑network experiment that:

- Learns a low‑dimensional **latent dynamical manifold** underlying a set of deterministic, noise‑free time series generated by the GEM‑LTE tidal model.  
- Demonstrates that this manifold is shared across many geo‑located indices and regional aggregates.  
- Produces latent dynamics whose temporal structure can be quantitatively compared to known tidal/astronomical forcings.

The end goal is not just accurate reconstruction, but **discovery** of a compact dynamical representation that is empirically consistent with a small set of quasi‑periodic drivers (e.g., tidal forcing).

---

## 2. Data Requirements

### 2.1 Time series

- Number of indices:
  - Local indices: **78**, each associated with a specific geographic location.
  - Regional aggregate indices: **12–14**, each representing an aggregate over a larger region.
- Total number of series:
  - \( M \approx 90\text{–}92 \).
- Temporal resolution:
  - **Monthly** values.
- Typical record length per series:
  - **≈ 1000 months** (≈ 83 years).
  - Individual indices may have different start/end times; document this explicitly.

### 2.2 Metadata

For each index:

- Unique identifier (string).  
- Type flag: `local` or `regional`.  
- Geographic coordinates:
  - Latitude (degrees).  
  - Longitude (degrees).  
- Region/basin label (e.g., Pacific, Atlantic, Indian, Arctic, Southern).  
- For regional indices:
  - List of constituent local indices (if applicable).

### 2.3 Proximity information

Define a **proximity metric** between indices based on geography:

- Distance measure:
  - Great‑circle distance \( d_{ij} \) between locations \(i\) and \(j\).  
- Proximity kernel:
  - \( W_{ij} = \exp(-d_{ij}^2 / (2\sigma^2)) \), with a chosen length scale \(\sigma\) (e.g., 1000–3000 km).  
- Optional refinement:
  - Set \( W_{ij} = 0 \) if indices belong to different ocean basins (as per basin labels).

This \( W \) will be used as an attention or graph bias to emphasize interactions among geographically nearby indices.

## 3. Experiment Design

### 3.1 Problem framing

- Input:
  - Sliding windows of multi‑index time series:
    - Window length: \( T_w = 180 \) months (15 years).  
    - Stride: 1–3 months between window centers.
- Task:
  - Learn a **low‑dimensional latent state** \( z_t \in \mathbb{R}^K \) at each time \( t \) that:
    - Encodes the shared dynamical state driving all indices.
    - Evolves according to a smooth, autonomous dynamical rule.
  - Decode \( z_t \) to reconstruct the full index vector \( X_t \in \mathbb{R}^M \).

### 3.2 Outputs

The experiment must provide:

1. A trained encoder–latent‑dynamics–decoder model.  
2. Time series of latent states \( z_t \) for all available months.  
3. Quantitative reconstruction metrics for all indices.  
4. Spectral diagnostics of the latent dynamics.  
5. A comparison between latent spectral peaks and known tidal / astronomical periods.

---

## 4. Model Architecture Requirements

### 4.1 Notation

- \( X_t \in \mathbb{R}^M \): vector of all indices at month \( t \).  
- \( z_t \in \mathbb{R}^K \): latent state at month \( t \).  
- \( T_w \): window length (months).  
- \( M \): number of indices (≈ 90–92).  
- \( K \): latent dimension (recommended 4–5).

### 4.2 Encoder

The encoder maps a window of multi‑index history to a latent state \( z_t \).

#### 4.2.1 Per‑index temporal encoding

For each index \( i \in \{1,\dots,M\} \):

- Input: time series segment \( x^{(i)}_{t-T_w+1:t} \in \mathbb{R}^{T_w} \).  
- Apply 1D convolutional layers over time:

  - 2–3 convolutional layers.  
  - Kernel size: 5 (or 3–7).  
  - Channels: 32 → 64.  
  - Activation: ReLU or tanh.  

- Output: hidden sequence \( h^{(i)}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{C} \), with \( C = 64 \).

Pool over time (e.g., global average pooling) to obtain a single vector for each index:

- \( p^{(i)} \in \mathbb{R}^{64} \).

#### 4.2.2 Incorporating geography (cross‑index interaction)

Construct extended index embeddings:

- Concatenate temporal and geographic features:
  - \( \tilde{p}^{(i)} = [p^{(i)}, \text{lat}_i, \text{lon}_i, \text{basin\_onehot}_i] \).

Apply a geo‑aware cross‑index layer:

- Option A: Multi‑head self‑attention.
  - Queries/keys/values derived from \( \tilde{p}^{(i)} \).  
  - 4–8 attention heads; head dimension ≈ 16.  
  - Add proximity bias based on \( W_{ij} \) to attention logits (indices closer in space get higher prior weight).

- Option B: Graph neural network layer.
  - Nodes: indices \(i\).  
  - Edges: weighted by \( W_{ij} \).  
  - One or more message‑passing steps.

Result: context‑enhanced embeddings \( q^{(i)} \in \mathbb{R}^{64} \) for each index.

#### 4.2.3 Global pooling to latent state

Aggregate across indices:

- Mean‑pool (or attention‑pool) across \( i \) to get a global vector:
  - \( \bar{q} \in \mathbb{R}^{64} \).  
- Apply a linear layer:
  - \( \bar{q} \rightarrow z_t \in \mathbb{R}^{K} \).

This defines the encoder function:

- \( z_t = \text{Enc}(X_{t-T_w+1:t}) \).

### 4.3 Latent dynamics module

Model the evolution of \( z_t \) as a low‑dimensional autonomous dynamical system.

- Dynamical map:
  - \( z_{t+1} = f_\theta(z_t) \).  
- Implementation:
  - Small GRU with hidden size ≈ 16, or  
  - 2‑layer MLP with ≈ 32 units per layer and tanh activation.

This module will be trained to map \( z_t \) to \( z_{t+1} \) consistently with the encoder.

### 4.4 Decoder

The decoder reconstructs indices from latent state \( z_t \).

#### 4.4.1 Simple global decoder

- Input: \( z_t \in \mathbb{R}^K \).  
- MLP with:
  - Layer 1: 64 units, tanh.  
  - Layer 2: 128 units, tanh.  
  - Output layer: \( M \) units (linear), giving \( \hat{X}_t \in \mathbb{R}^M \).

#### 4.4.2 Optional geo‑aware decoder

Alternative (if needed):

- For each index \( i \):
  - Concatenate \( z_t \) with index‑specific geo features:
    - \( [z_t, \text{lat}_i, \text{lon}_i, \text{basin\_onehot}_i] \).  
  - Apply a small shared MLP to produce \( \hat{x}^{(i)}_t \).  

This allows the decoder to express geographic variation in the projection of the latent manifold.

## 5. Training Procedure

### 5.1 Windowing

- Generate training samples by sliding a window of length \( T_w = 180 \) months:
  - For center time \( t \), input window \( X_{t-T_w+1:t} \).  
  - Use stride 1–3 months between centers.

### 5.2 Batching

- Batch size: choose to fit GPU/CPU memory (e.g., 16–64 windows per batch).  
- Ensure that multiple regions and index types are represented in each batch.

### 5.3 Loss functions

Total loss:

\[
\mathcal{L}
= \mathcal{L}_\text{recon}
+ \lambda_\text{dyn} \mathcal{L}_\text{dyn}
+ \lambda_\text{smooth} \mathcal{L}_\text{smooth}
+ \lambda_\text{spec} \mathcal{L}_\text{spec}
\]

#### 5.3.1 Reconstruction loss

For each center time \( t \) in the batch:

\[
\mathcal{L}_\text{recon}
= \frac{1}{M} \sum_{i=1}^M (X_{t,i} - \hat{X}_{t,i})^2
\]

- Optionally reweight regional indices slightly higher if desired.

#### 5.3.2 Latent dynamics consistency

For overlapping windows:

- Compute \( z_t = \text{Enc}(X_{t-T_w+1:t}) \).  
- Compute \( z_{t+1} = \text{Enc}(X_{t+1-T_w+1:t+1}) \).  
- Apply:

\[
\mathcal{L}_\text{dyn}
= \frac{1}{N_t} \sum_{t} \| f_\theta(z_t) - z_{t+1} \|^2
\]

Where \( N_t \) is the number of time pairs in the batch.

#### 5.3.3 Latent smoothness loss

\[
\mathcal{L}_\text{smooth}
= \frac{1}{N_t} \sum_{t} \| z_{t+1} - z_t \|^2
\]

This discourages high‑frequency variation in the latent dynamics.

#### 5.3.4 Spectral sparsity regularization

To encourage a small number of dominant periods in each latent dimension:

1. Periodically (or via a differentiable layer), collect a sequence of latent states \( z_t \) over a longer span (e.g., 512 or more time steps).  
2. Compute the discrete Fourier transform for each dimension \( k \):
   - \( Z^{(k)}_f = \text{FFT}(z^{(k)}_t) \).  
3. Define normalized spectral amplitudes:
   - \( p^{(k)}_f = \frac{|Z^{(k)}_f|}{\sum_{f'} |Z^{(k)}_{f'}|} \).  
4. Compute spectral entropy:
   - \( \mathcal{H}^{(k)} = - \sum_f p^{(k)}_f \log p^{(k)}_f \).  

Then:

\[
\mathcal{L}_\text{spec}
= \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^{K} \mathcal{H}^{(k)}
\]

Lower entropy corresponds to fewer dominant spectral peaks.

### 5.4 Hyperparameters

Initial recommended values:

- Latent dimension: \( K = 4 \)–5.  
- \( \lambda_\text{dyn} = 1.0 \).  
- \( \lambda_\text{smooth} = 0.1 \).  
- \( \lambda_\text{spec} = 0.01 \).  
- Optimizer: Adam or similar, standard learning rate (e.g., 1e‑3, tuned via validation).  
- Early stopping based on validation reconstruction loss and stability of latent spectra.

## 6. Evaluation and Diagnostics

### 6.1 Reconstruction quality

For each index:

- Compute:
  - RMSE and MAE between \( X_{t,i} \) and \( \hat{X}_{t,i} \).  
  - Pearson correlation over the full time span.  
- Verify that both local and regional indices are reconstructed with high fidelity.

### 6.2 Latent dimensionality

- Confirm that \( K \) is sufficient:
  - If reconstruction error remains high and does not improve with training, consider increasing \( K \) by 1–2.  
  - If \( K \) is too large, latent dynamics may become noisy and spectral entropy may remain high; consider reducing \( K \) or increasing regularization.

### 6.3 Spectral analysis of latent dynamics

After training:

1. Run the encoder over the entire time span to obtain \( z_t \) for all months.  
2. For each latent dimension:
   - Compute the power spectrum of \( z^{(k)}_t \).  
   - Identify dominant periods (peaks).

3. Check for:
   - A small set of clearly identifiable spectral peaks in each \( z^{(k)} \).  
   - Consistency of peak frequencies across training runs and random initializations.

### 6.4 Comparison to known tidal / astronomical periods

Define a library of candidate physical periods (from external astronomical data):

- Principal tidal constituents and long‑period modulations (e.g., M2, S2, K1, O1, nodal 18.6‑year, perigee‑related cycles, and their relevant combinations).

For each latent spectral peak:

- Identify the nearest physical period in the library.  
- Record:
  - Latent period \( P_\text{latent} \).  
  - Nearest physical period \( P_\text{phys} \).  
  - Relative difference \( |P_\text{latent} - P_\text{phys}| / P_\text{phys} \).  

Assess whether:

- The same small set of physical periods systematically appears across latent dimensions and runs.  
- The relative differences are small enough to be consistent with model and sampling uncertainties.

### 6.5 Optional phase regression

As an additional test:

- Construct time series of known astronomical phase variables \( \phi_j(t) \) for key constituents.  
- Regress latent components \( z^{(k)}_t \) onto sin/cos of these phases:
  - \( z^{(k)}_t \approx \sum_j \alpha_{kj} \sin(\phi_j(t)) + \beta_{kj} \cos(\phi_j(t)) \).  
- Evaluate:
  - Coefficients of determination \( R^2 \).  
  - Stability of fitted phase relationships across runs.

High and stable \( R^2 \) values support the interpretation of \( z_t \) as a nonlinear coordinate transform of tidal phase variables.

## 7. Reproducibility and Reporting

### 7.1 Configuration logging

Record and publish:

- Model architecture details (layer sizes, activations, attention or graph specifics).  
- Latent dimension \( K \).  
- Loss weights \( \lambda_\text{dyn}, \lambda_\text{smooth}, \lambda_\text{spec} \).  
- Window length \( T_w \) and stride.  
- Random seeds used.

### 7.2 Code and data

Provide:

- Scripts/notebooks for:
  - Preprocessing GEM‑LTE outputs into the required format.  
  - Constructing proximity matrix \( W_{ij} \).  
  - Training the NN model.  
  - Computing latent spectra and comparison to physical periods.  
- Configuration files (YAML/JSON) defining all experiment parameters.

### 7.3 Results presentation

Document:

- Reconstruction metrics for each index (table or figure).  
- Example time‑series plots (observed vs reconstructed) for representative local and regional indices.  
- Power spectra of latent dimensions, with candidate tidal periods overlaid.  
- Summary statistics of alignment between latent and physical periods.  
- Discussion of robustness across:
  - Different initializations.  
  - Slight variations in hyperparameters.  
  - Inclusion/exclusion of subsets of indices.

---

## 8. Success Criteria

The experiment will be considered a successful **latent manifold discovery** if:

1. A low‑dimensional latent state \( z_t \) (with small K) reconstructs all indices with high accuracy.  
2. Latent dynamics are smooth and show a limited number of strong spectral peaks.  
3. The dominant latent periods consistently align with a small set of known tidal/astronomical periods within reasonable tolerances.  
4. These findings are robust to moderate changes in architecture and training details.

Such a result would support the claim that the neural network has *empirically recovered* a hidden quasi‑periodic dynamical manifold consistent with tidal forcing, using only the GEM‑LTE‑generated indices and their geographic structure.
